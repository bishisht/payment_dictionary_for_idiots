\dictentry{EFT Switch}{/ee eff tee switch/, \textit{n.}}{
Electronic Funds Transfer Switch. A computer system that acts as a central hub connecting different banks, card networks, and payment providers together. When you use your debit card at an ATM or store, the EFT Switch routes your transaction request to your bank and brings back the approval or decline response. Think of it as a telephone switchboard operator, but for money transfers.

\example{When you withdraw cash from an ATM that belongs to a different bank than yours, an EFT Switch connects your bank to that ATM's bank to process the transaction.}

\crossref{\textbf{ATM}, \textbf{Payment Gateway}, \textbf{Authorization}}
}
\indexentry{EFT Switch}

\dictentry{EMV}{/ee em vee/, \textit{abbr.}}{
Europay, Mastercard, and Visa. A global standard for credit and debit cards that use computer chips to store and protect payment information. EMV cards (also called chip cards) are more secure than old magnetic stripe cards because the chip creates a unique code for each transaction that cannot be reused if stolen. The chip is the small gold or silver square you see on modern payment cards.

\example{When you insert your credit card into a payment terminal at a store and wait a few seconds, you're using EMV chip technology instead of swiping the magnetic stripe.}

\crossref{\textbf{Chip Card}, \textbf{Magnetic Stripe}, \textbf{Tokenization}, \textbf{POS}}

\etymology{Named after the three companies (Europay, Mastercard, Visa) that originally created this standard in the 1990s. EMV chips became widely adopted in the United States after 2015.}
}
